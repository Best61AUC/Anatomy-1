Radiological Anatomy: Fundamentals

Bone Structure & Classification

Parts of a Long Bone

  • Epiphysis: The expanded end of a long bone, which forms a joint with another bone. It is covered by a layer of articular cartilage.
  • Diaphysis: The main shaft of the bone, located between the two epiphyses.
  • Metaphysis: The region between the epiphysis and diaphysis. In growing bones, it contains the epiphyseal disk (growth plate), a band of cartilage responsible for bone lengthening.

Bone Histology (Tissue Types)

  • Compact Bone (Cortex): A dense, tightly packed tissue that forms the hard outer wall of the diaphysis, creating a rigid tube. It contains no visible spaces.
  • Spongy Bone (Medulla): Located inside the compact bone, forming a hollow chamber. It consists of a network of branching bony plates called trabeculae. The spaces between trabeculae are filled with bone marrow, which produces blood cells.

Bone Classification by Shape

  • Long Bones: Have a longitudinal axis and expanded ends (e.g., bones of the arms and legs like the femur and humerus).
  • Short Bones: Roughly cube-shaped (e.g., bones of the wrist (carpals) and ankle (tarsals)).
  • Flat Bones: Plate-like structures with broad surfaces (e.g., scapula, ribs, most skull bones).
  • Irregular Bones: Have complex, varied shapes that do not fit into other categories (e.g., vertebrae, some facial bones).
  • Sesamoid Bones: Small bones that develop within tendons, typically near joints, to reduce friction and modify pressure (e.g., the patella or kneecap).

Bone Membranes and Growth

Membranes

  • Periosteum: A tough, fibrous tissue layer that covers the entire surface of a bone, except for the articular cartilage on the epiphyses. It is rich in nerves and provides attachment points for tendons and ligaments.
  • Endosteum: A thin membrane that lines the medullary cavity (the hollow part of the diaphysis).

Bone Growth (Ossification)

  • Most skeletal bones are endochondral bones, meaning they develop from a cartilage model.
  • Primary Ossification Center: The process begins in the center of the diaphysis, where cartilage is replaced by bone tissue.
  • Secondary Ossification Centers: Appear later in the epiphyses, with bone formation spreading outwards.
  • The epiphyseal disk (metaphysis) remains as cartilage, allowing the bone to grow in length as long as its cells are active. Once growth is complete, it ossifies.

Joints (Articulations)

A joint is formed where two or more bones meet. They are classified by their structure.

A) Synovial Joints

The most common type of joint, allowing for significant movement. They have a characteristic structure:

  • Articular Cartilage: A thin layer of hyaline cartilage covering the ends of the bones within the joint to reduce friction.
  • Joint Capsule: A two-layered structure that encloses the joint.
    • Outer fibrous layer: Attaches to the periosteum of the bones.
    • Inner synovial membrane: Lines the joint cavity but does not cover the articular cartilage. It secretes synovial fluid.
  • Synovial Cavity: A closed sac formed by the synovial membrane, containing synovial fluid.
  • Synovial Fluid: A clear, viscous fluid that lubricates and moistens the cartilaginous surfaces, reducing friction and absorbing shock.

Classification of Synovial Joints

Synovial joints are further classified by the shape of their articulating surfaces and the range of motion they permit.

Joint Type Description Movement Examples
Ball and Socket A rounded head of one bone fits into a cup-shaped cavity of another. Allows all types of movement (multiaxial), including rotation. Hip and shoulder joints.
Condyloid An ovoid end of one bone fits into an elliptical cavity of another. Allows all movements except rotation. Metacarpophalangeal joints (knuckles).
Gliding (Plane) Articular surfaces are mostly flat. Allows only sliding or gliding movements. Joints between carpal bones (wrist).
Hinge A convex surface fits into a concave surface. Allows movement in one plane (like a door hinge), i.e., flexion and extension. Elbow and knee joints.
Pivot A cylindrical surface rotates within a ring of bone and ligament. Allows rotational movement around a central axis. Atlanto-axial joint (between C1 and C2 vertebrae).
Saddle Each surface has both concave and convex areas, fitting together like a rider on a saddle. Allows a variety of movements, including back-and-forth and side-to-side. First carpometacarpal joint (base of the thumb).

B) Fibrous Joints

Bones are in close contact and held together by dense fibrous connective tissue. These joints allow for no or very limited movement.

  • Example: The sutures of the skull.
  • In infants, these areas are membranous regions called fontanels. Fontanels allow the skull to change shape during birth and accommodate brain growth. They later ossify and are replaced by sutures in adults.

C) Cartilaginous Joints

Bones are connected by hyaline cartilage or fibrocartilage. These joints allow for slight movement.

  • Example 1: The joint between the sternum and the first rib (connected by costal cartilage).
  • Example 2: Joints between vertebral bodies, which are separated by cartilaginous intervertebral disks.
  • Example 3: The symphysis pubis, which is the junction between the two hip bones in the pelvis.
  • Example 4: The sacroiliac joints between the sacrum and the hip bones.

The Upper Limb

The upper limb is designed for a high degree of mobility and freedom of movement. It is attached to the trunk by the shoulder girdle, with the only bony articulation being the sternoclavicular joint.

  • Arm: The region between the shoulder and elbow joints.
  • Forearm: The region between the elbow and wrist joints.
  • Hand: The region distal to the wrist joint.

I. The Hand

The skeleton of the hand consists of three regions.

1. Carpal Bones (Wrist)

Eight small bones arranged in two rows of four.

  • Proximal Row (from lateral to medial): Scaphoid, Lunate, Triquetral, Pisiform.
  • Distal Row (from lateral to medial): Trapezium, Trapezoid, Capitate, Hamate.
  • Key Bones:
    • The Scaphoid is the largest bone in the proximal row and is the most frequently fractured carpal bone.
    • The Capitate is the central and largest carpal bone, articulating with the third metacarpal.

2. Metacarpal Bones (Palm)

Five small long bones, each with a distal head, a shaft, and an expanded base. They are numbered 1-5, starting from the thumb.

3. Phalanges (Fingers)

There are 14 phalanges. Each finger has three (proximal, middle, distal), while the thumb has two (proximal, distal).

Wrist Joint (Radiocarpal Joint)

Formed by the articulation of the distal ends of the radius and ulna with the scaphoid, lunate, and triquetral carpal bones.

II. The Forearm

Contains two long bones: the Radius (lateral, on the thumb side) and the Ulna (medial, on the pinky side).

1. The Radius

  • The distal end is broader than the proximal end.
  • Proximal End: Features a disk-like head (articulating with the ulna and humerus), a neck, and the radial tuberosity.
  • Distal End: The widest part of the bone. Features the radial styloid process (a lateral projection) and the ulnar notch (for articulation with the ulna's head).

2. The Ulna

  • The medial bone of the forearm.
  • Proximal End: Hook-like shape, concave forwards. Features the large olecranon process (point of the elbow) and the coronoid process. These form the trochlear notch, which articulates with the humerus.
  • Distal End: Features a small head and a short ulnar styloid process.

Elbow Joint

A complex hinge joint involving two articulations:

  1. Between the trochlea of the humerus and the trochlear notch of the ulna.
  2. Between the capitulum of the humerus and the radial head.

III. The Arm: The Humerus

The humerus is the single long bone of the arm, and it is the longest and largest bone in the upper limb.

A) Proximal End

  • Head: A smooth, rounded structure that articulates with the glenoid cavity of the scapula to form the shoulder joint.
  • Anatomical Neck: A slight constriction immediately distal to the head.
  • Greater & Lesser Tubercles (Tuberosities): Projections that serve as attachment sites for rotator cuff muscles. The greater is lateral, the lesser is anterior.

B) Shaft

Cylindrical proximally and flattened distally.

C) Distal End

A wide end that articulates with the radius and ulna at the elbow.

  • Capitulum: A lateral, rounded process that articulates with the head of the radius.
  • Trochlea: A medial, pulley-like process that articulates with the trochlear notch of the ulna.
  • Medial and Lateral Epicondyles: Projections on either side of the distal end for muscle attachment. The medial epicondyle is more prominent ("funny bone").
  • Fossae: Depressions that accommodate the processes of the ulna during elbow movement (Olecranon, Coronoid, and Radial fossae).

IV. The Shoulder Girdle

Connects the upper limb to the trunk. It is formed by two bones: the anterior Clavicle and the posterior Scapula.

1. The Scapula (Shoulder Blade)

A large, flat, triangular bone located on the posterior thorax, typically spanning from the 2nd to the 7th ribs.

  • Body: The main flat, triangular part.
  • Glenoid Cavity (Fossa): A shallow depression on the lateral angle that articulates with the head of the humerus to form the shoulder joint.
  • Processes (Projections):
    • Spine: A prominent ridge on the posterior surface.
    • Acromion: The lateral extension of the spine that projects forward. It articulates with the clavicle (acromioclavicular joint) and forms the palpable "tip" of the shoulder.
    • Coracoid Process: A hook-like projection on the anterior surface, near the head, for muscle attachment.

2. The Clavicle (Collarbone)

An S-shaped bone that extends horizontally across the neck, connecting the sternum to the acromion.

  • Function: Transmits the weight of the limb to the axial skeleton.
  • Medial End (Sternal End): Articulates with the manubrium of the sternum (sternoclavicular joint).
  • Lateral End (Acromial End): Flat and articulates with the acromion (acromioclavicular joint).

The Lower Limb

The primary functions of the lower limb are weight-bearing and locomotion. Consequently, its bones are larger and stronger, and its joints are more stable than those of the upper limb.

  • Thigh: The region between the hip and knee joints.
  • Leg: The region between the knee and ankle joints.
  • Foot: The region distal to the ankle joint.

I. The Foot

The skeleton of the foot is divided into three parts and is arranged into arches to support and distribute body weight.

A) Tarsal Bones (Ankle)

Seven bones that occupy the proximal half of the foot.

  • Key Bones:
    • Talus: Sits superiorly, articulating with the tibia and fibula to form the ankle joint.
    • Calcaneus: The largest tarsal bone, forming the heel.
  • Other Tarsals: Navicular, Cuboid, and three Cuneiform bones (Medial, Intermediate, Lateral).

B) Metatarsal Bones

Five bones that connect the tarsals to the phalanges. The first metatarsal (at the base of the big toe) is the shortest and thickest, playing a crucial role in weight-bearing.

C) Phalanges (Toes)

Fourteen phalanges. The big toe (hallux) has two, while the other four toes each have three. They are shorter than the phalanges of the hand.

Ankle Joint

A hinge joint formed by the articulation of the talus with the tibia and fibula. The medial malleolus of the tibia and the lateral malleolus of the fibula form a deep recess that provides great stability.

II. The Leg

Contains two long bones: the Tibia (medial) and the Fibula (lateral).

1. The Tibia (Shin Bone)

The larger, medial, and primary weight-bearing bone of the leg. It is the second longest bone in the body after the femur.

  • Proximal End: Expanded transversely into medial and lateral condyles, which articulate with the femoral condyles at the knee joint. The tibial tuberosity is a rough anterior projection for ligament attachment.
  • Shaft: Triangular in cross-section with a sharp anterior border that is easily palpable under the skin.
  • Distal End: Projects inferomedially to form the medial malleolus (the inner ankle bone).

2. The Fibula

A slender bone located lateral to the tibia. It is not directly involved in weight-bearing but serves as an important site for muscle attachment and helps stabilize the ankle joint.

  • Head: The proximal part, which articulates with the lateral condyle of the tibia.
  • Shaft: Long and slender.
  • Distal End (Lateral Malleolus): Projects distally and posteriorly to form the outer ankle bone.

The Knee Joint

The largest and one of the most complex joints in the body. It is a modified hinge joint.

Key Structures

  • Articulating Bones: Femoral condyles, tibial condyles, and the patella.
  • Menisci (Semilunar Cartilages): Two C-shaped fibrocartilage discs (medial and lateral) that sit on the tibial condyles.
    • Functions: Deepen the articular surface of the tibia, act as shock absorbers, and assist in lubrication.
  • Ligaments: Provide crucial stability.
    • Collateral Ligaments: Tibial (Medial) and Fibular (Lateral) collateral ligaments prevent side-to-side movement.
    • Cruciate Ligaments: Anterior Cruciate Ligament (ACL) and Posterior Cruciate Ligament (PCL) cross inside the joint, preventing forward/backward displacement of the tibia on the femur.
    • Patellar Ligament: Connects the patella to the tibial tuberosity.

The Patella (Kneecap)

The largest sesamoid bone in the body. It is embedded in the tendon of the quadriceps femoris muscle and lies anterior to the knee joint. Function: Increases the mechanical advantage (leverage) of the quadriceps muscle during knee extension.

III. The Thigh: The Femur

The femur is the single bone of the thigh. It is the longest, heaviest, and strongest bone in the human body.

1. Proximal End

  • Head: A smooth, rounded head that articulates with the acetabulum of the hip bone to form the hip joint.
  • Neck: Connects the head to the shaft at an angle of about 125°. This is a common site for fractures, especially in the elderly.
  • Greater and Lesser Trochanters: Large projections at the junction of the neck and shaft that serve as powerful attachment points for muscles of the hip and thigh.

2. Shaft

Cylindrical and bows slightly forward (anteriorly).

3. Distal End

Widely expanded to transmit weight to the tibia.

  • Medial and Lateral Condyles: Large, smooth surfaces that articulate with the tibial condyles to form the knee joint.
  • Intercondylar Fossa: A deep depression on the posterior side that separates the two condyles.

The Hip Joint

A classic ball-and-socket synovial joint known for its strength and stability. It is formed by the articulation of the femoral head with the deep, cup-shaped acetabulum of the hip bone. The joint is reinforced by a strong fibrous capsule and powerful ligaments (Iliofemoral, Ischiofemoral, Pubofemoral).

The Pelvis

The pelvis is a strong, basin-shaped ring of bones that connects the vertebral column to the femurs. Its primary functions are to transmit body weight to the lower limbs, support pelvic organs, and provide attachment for muscles.

It is formed by the two hip bones (os coxae) and the sacrum.

The Hip Bone (Os Coxae)

A large, irregular bone that is constricted centrally and expanded above and below. In adults, it is formed by the fusion of three separate bones: the Ilium, Ischium, and Pubis. These three bones meet and fuse in the acetabulum, the deep socket for the femoral head.

1. The Ilium

  • The largest and most superior part of the hip bone.
  • Features the iliac crest, the curved superior border that you can feel at your waistline.
  • Articulates posteriorly with the sacrum at the sacroiliac joint.

2. The Ischium

  • The inferoposterior part of the hip bone.
  • Features the prominent ischial tuberosity, which is the rough area you sit on ("sitting bone").

3. The Pubis

  • The anterior part of the hip bone.
  • The two pubic bones meet in the midline at the symphysis pubis, a cartilaginous joint.
  • The ischium and pubis together form the obturator foramen, a large opening for nerves and blood vessels.

The Thorax (Chest)

The bony thorax, or rib cage, is a protective framework for the vital organs of the thoracic cavity (heart, lungs) and plays a crucial role in breathing.

The bony thorax is formed by:

  • The Sternum (anteriorly)
  • 12 pairs of Ribs (laterally)
  • 12 Thoracic Vertebrae (posteriorly)

1. The Sternum (Breastbone)

A narrow, flat bone about 15 cm long, located in the anterior midline of the chest. It is divided into three parts:

  • Manubrium: The upper, broadest part. It articulates with the clavicles and the first two pairs of ribs.
  • Body: The longest part, articulating with ribs 2 through 7. The junction between the manubrium and body is the sternal angle, an important clinical landmark.
  • Xiphoid Process: The small, cartilaginous distal part.

2. The Ribs

Twelve pairs of long, arched bones.

  • True Ribs (1-7): Attach directly to the sternum via their own costal cartilage.
  • False Ribs (8-10): Their costal cartilages attach to the cartilage of the rib above, not directly to the sternum.
  • Floating Ribs (11-12): Do not attach to the sternum at all.

The Thoracic Cavity

The space enclosed by the bony thorax and the diaphragm (the muscular floor).

Contents of the Thoracic Cavity

It includes the two pleural cavities (containing the lungs) and the mediastinum.

1. The Mediastinum

The central compartment of the thoracic cavity, located between the lungs. It is divided into superior and inferior sections.

  • Superior Mediastinum: Contains the aortic arch, large veins (e.g., SVC), trachea, and esophagus.
  • Inferior Mediastinum: Further divided into:
    • Anterior: Contains fat and lymph nodes.
    • Middle: Contains the heart, great vessels, and tracheal bifurcation.
    • Posterior: Contains the descending aorta, esophagus, and thoracic duct.

2. The Lungs

The primary organs of respiration, located on either side of the mediastinum.

  • Each lung is conical, with an apex (superiorly) and a base (inferiorly, resting on the diaphragm).
  • The hilum is the region on the medial surface where bronchi, blood vessels, and nerves enter and exit the lung.
  • Lobes and Fissures:
    • The Right Lung is slightly larger and is divided by the oblique and horizontal fissures into three lobes (upper, middle, lower).
    • The Left Lung is smaller (to accommodate the heart) and is divided by the oblique fissure into two lobes (upper, lower).

The Vertebral Column (Spine)

The vertebral column is the central axis of the skeleton, providing support, flexibility, and protection for the spinal cord.

Structure of a Typical Vertebra

A typical vertebra consists of two main parts:

  1. Vertebral Body: The thick, anterior, weight-bearing part. The bodies increase in size from the cervical to the lumbar region to support increasing weight.
  2. Vertebral Arch: The posterior part that encloses the vertebral foramen (the space for the spinal cord). It is formed by:
    • Two Pedicles: Short bars that project from the body.
    • Two Laminae: Flat plates that fuse in the midline.

Vertebral Processes (7 total)

  • Spinous Process (1): Projects posteriorly from the junction of the laminae.
  • Transverse Processes (2): Project laterally from the junction of the pedicles and laminae.
  • Articular Processes (4): Two superior and two inferior processes, which form synovial joints with the adjacent vertebrae.

Joints of the Vertebral Column

  • Between Vertebral Bodies: Cartilaginous joints connected by intervertebral discs (composed of an inner nucleus pulposus and outer annulus fibrosus).
  • Between Vertebral Arches: Synovial joints between the articular processes.

Regional Characteristics of Vertebrae

1. Cervical Vertebrae (C1-C7)

  • Features:
    • Small body, large triangular vertebral foramen.
    • Have a transverse foramen in each transverse process for the passage of the vertebral artery.
    • Spinous processes are often short and bifid (split).
  • Atypical Cervical Vertebrae:
    • C1 (Atlas): Has no body or spinous process. It is a ring of bone that articulates with the occipital condyles of the skull (allowing the "yes" motion).
    • C2 (Axis): Has a superior projection called the odontoid process (dens), which acts as a pivot for the rotation of the atlas (allowing the "no" motion).
    • C7 (Vertebra Prominens): Has a long, non-bifid spinous process that is easily palpable.

2. Thoracic Vertebrae (T1-T12)

  • Features:
    • Larger than cervical vertebrae with a heart-shaped body.
    • Have costal facets on their bodies and transverse processes for articulation with the ribs.
    • Spinous processes are long and point inferiorly.

3. Lumbar Vertebrae (L1-L5)

  • Features:
    • The largest and strongest vertebrae to support the most body weight.
    • Body is massive and kidney-shaped.
    • Spinous processes are short, thick, and project straight backwards.

The Sacrum and Coccyx

The Sacrum

  • A large, triangular bone formed by the fusion of 5 sacral vertebrae.
  • Forms the posterior wall of the bony pelvis.
  • Articulates superiorly with L5 and laterally with the iliac bones of the pelvis at the sacroiliac joints.
  • The anterior border of the first sacral vertebra is called the sacral promontory, an important obstetric landmark.

The Coccyx (Tailbone)

  • A small, triangular bone formed by the fusion of 3-5 (usually 4) coccygeal vertebrae.
  • Articulates with the apex of the sacrum.

The Skull

The skull is the most complex bony structure in the body. It encloses the brain, houses the organs of special senses, and surrounds the openings of the digestive and respiratory tracts.

The bones are subdivided into the Calvaria (Cranium) and the Facial Skeleton.

I. The Calvaria (Cranium)

A box-like structure composed of 8 bones that protect the brain.

  • Unpaired Bones: Frontal, Occipital, Sphenoid, Ethmoid.
  • Paired Bones: Parietal (2), Temporal (2).

1. Frontal Bone

Forms the forehead and the roof of the orbits (eye sockets).

2. Parietal Bones (Paired)

Form the majority of the superior and lateral walls of the calvaria. They articulate with each other at the sagittal suture.

3. Occipital Bone

Forms most of the posterior and base of the skull. It contains the foramen magnum, the large opening through which the spinal cord connects to the brainstem. It also has occipital condyles for articulation with the C1 vertebra (atlas).

The Calvaria (Cranium) (Continued)

4. Temporal Bones (Paired)

Form parts of the sides and base of the skull. Each temporal bone has several important parts and processes:

  • Squamous Part: The flat, fan-like superior part.
  • Zygomatic Process: Projects anteriorly to articulate with the zygomatic bone, forming the zygomatic arch (cheekbone).
  • Petrous Part: A dense, pyramid-shaped part that houses the internal and middle ear organs.
  • Mastoid Process: A prominent bump posterior to the ear, for muscle attachment.
  • Styloid Process: A thin, pointed projection inferiorly.
  • Mandibular Fossa: Articulates with the mandible to form the temporomandibular joint (TMJ).

5. Sphenoid Bone

A complex, bat-shaped bone that forms the anterior base of the skull. It is considered the "keystone" of the cranium because it articulates with all other cranial bones.

  • Key feature: The sella turcica, a depression on its superior surface that houses the pituitary gland.

6. Ethmoid Bone

A delicate bone located between the sphenoid and nasal bones. It forms part of the nasal septum, the roof of the nasal cavity, and the medial wall of the orbits.

II. The Skeleton of the Face

The facial skeleton is formed by 14 bones (most are paired).

1. Mandible (Unpaired)

The U-shaped bone of the lower jaw. It is the largest and strongest facial bone and the only freely movable bone of the skull (excluding the ossicles of the ear).

  • Body: The horizontal part that contains the sockets for the lower teeth.
  • Rami (2): The two vertical parts that ascend from the body. Each ramus has:
    • A posterior Condylar Process (which articulates with the temporal bone at the TMJ).
    • An anterior Coronoid Process (for muscle attachment).
  • The point where the body and ramus meet is the mandibular angle.

2. Maxillae (Paired)

The two maxillae fuse medially to form the upper jaw and the central portion of the facial skeleton. They are the "keystone" bones of the face, articulating with all other facial bones except the mandible.

3. Zygomatic Bones (Paired)

The cheekbones. They form the prominences of the cheeks and the inferolateral margins of the orbits.

4. Nasal Bones (Paired)

Form the bridge of the nose.

5. Other Facial Bones

Lacrimal bones, palatine bones, vomer, and inferior nasal conchae complete the facial skeleton.

The Paranasal Sinuses

Paired, air-filled cavities located within specific bones of the skull. They are lined with mucous membranes and open into the nasal cavity.

Functions: Lighten the skull, warm and moisten inhaled air, and act as resonance chambers for speech.

The four pairs of sinuses are:

  1. Frontal Sinuses:
    • Located in the frontal bone, superior to the orbits.
    • Usually develop after age 7 and reach full size after puberty.
  2. Maxillary Sinuses:
    • The largest of the sinuses, located in the maxillary bones.
    • Their floor is near the roots of the upper teeth, which can lead to toothaches during sinus infections.
  3. Ethmoid Sinuses (Air Cells):
    • A collection of small, numerous air cells located in the ethmoid bone between the nasal cavity and the orbit.
  4. Sphenoid Sinuses:
    • Located in the body of the sphenoid bone, posterior to the upper nasal cavity and inferior to the sella turcica.

The Urinary System

The urinary system is responsible for filtering waste products from the blood and excreting them as urine, while also regulating water, electrolyte, and acid-base balance.

The urinary system consists of:

  • A pair of Kidneys
  • A pair of Ureters
  • A Urinary Bladder
  • A Urethra

The Kidneys

  • Bean-shaped organs located retroperitoneally (behind the peritoneum) on the posterior abdominal wall.
  • The right kidney is typically slightly lower than the left due to the position of the liver.
  • The medial, concave surface has a slit called the renal hilum, where the ureter, renal artery, and renal vein enter and exit.

Internal Structure of the Kidney

  • Renal Cortex: The outer, superficial layer.
  • Renal Medulla: The inner region, composed of cone-shaped masses called renal pyramids.
  • Pelvi-calyceal System: Urine drains from the apex of each pyramid (renal papilla) into a minor calyx. Several minor calyces merge to form a major calyx. The major calyces then drain into the funnel-shaped renal pelvis, which is continuous with the ureter.

Components of the Urinary Tract (Continued)

The Ureters

  • Slender tubes, about 25 cm long, that transport urine from the renal pelvis of each kidney to the urinary bladder.
  • Urine is moved by muscular peristaltic waves.

The Urinary Bladder (UB)

  • A hollow, muscular, distensible organ located in the pelvis, just posterior to the symphysis pubis.
  • Serves as a temporary storage reservoir for urine.
  • In males, it lies anterior to the rectum. In females, it lies anterior to the uterus.

The Urethra

  • A muscular tube that conveys urine from the bladder to the outside of the body.
  • Female Urethra: Short (about 4 cm) and serves only a urinary function.
  • Male Urethra: Long (about 18 cm) and serves both urinary and reproductive functions (transports urine and semen). It is divided into three regions:
    1. Prostatic Urethra: Passes through the prostate gland.
    2. Membranous Urethra: Passes through the muscles of the pelvic floor.
    3. Penile (Spongy) Urethra: Passes through the penis.

The Gastrointestinal (GI) Tract

The digestive system is composed of the alimentary canal and accessory organs. Its function is to digest food, absorb nutrients, and eliminate waste.

1. The Alimentary Canal (GI Tract)

A continuous muscular tube, about 9 meters long, extending from the mouth to the anus. It includes the mouth, pharynx, esophagus, stomach, small intestine, and large intestine.

2. Accessory Organs

These organs release secretions into the alimentary canal to aid digestion. They include the salivary glands, liver, gallbladder, and pancreas.

The Pharynx (Throat)

A cavity posterior to the mouth that connects the nasal and oral cavities to the larynx and esophagus. It serves as a passageway for both food and air.

  • Nasopharynx: Superior part, posterior to the nasal cavity. Passageway for air only.
  • Oropharynx: Middle part, posterior to the oral cavity. Passageway for both food and air.
  • Laryngopharynx: Inferior part, continuous with the esophagus. Passageway for both food and air.

The Esophagus

A muscular tube about 25 cm long that provides a passageway for food and fluids from the pharynx to the stomach. It lies posterior to the trachea and travels through the mediastinum, piercing the diaphragm to enter the stomach.
